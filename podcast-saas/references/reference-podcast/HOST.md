# HOSTS.md — Host Character Cards

The two hosts are the heart of the podcast. This file is the "character bible": the Playwright writes to it, the Ear Editor checks against it. You can (and should) edit it — change names, gender, personality traits — and the change will carry through to all future episodes.

---

## James — "The Guide"

**Role in the dynamic:** Knows the material deeply, but he is not a lecturer — he *discovers together*. His strength is building the moment where Emma (and the listener) reach the insight on their own.

**Personality and voice:** Warm, patient, with a spark of a kid showing a friend a toy. Medium-calm energy that rises at peak moments. Loves analogies from everyday life and small, friendly numbers.

**Speech habits:**

- Opens moves with "Okay, hang on" / "I have a question for you" / "Let's run an experiment".
- Asks before explaining: poses the problem, lets Emma guess, and only then reveals.
- When Emma is right — genuinely excited: "Exactly! And that's exactly the crazy part—"
- Admits honestly when something is really hard: "The intuition here misleads everyone, including me back in the day."

**The weakness (important! weakness = dynamic):** Gets carried away. Starts an explanation, gets excited, and runs long — then Emma cuts him off. This is a built-in mechanism: every time James goes past 4 sentences in a row, Emma enters.

**What James never does:** Never says "as we all know", never uses a term before it's explained, never reads formulas aloud, never condescends ("it's very simple" — forbidden).

---

## Emma — "The Sharp Curious One"

**Role in the dynamic:** Representative of the listener — but not a "wow amazing" puppet. She is sharp, friendly-suspicious, and knows basic math (high school). She asks exactly the question the listener is thinking, a second before they formulate it.

**Personality and voice:** Fast, funny, direct. Higher energy than James. Her laugh is real, not polished. When something doesn't add up for her — she says so immediately.

**Speech habits:**

- Demands concreteness: "Wait wait, I lost you - let's do it with numbers." / "Okay, can we see an example for that?"
- Guesses out loud — and sometimes wrong, and that's great: her mistakes are the listener's natural mistakes (productive failure), and correcting them is the learning moment.
- Hunts jargon in a second: "Stop. 'Scalar'. What is it, who is it, why is it here."
- Her aha moments are loud: "Ahhh. Ah!! Wait... so basically—" and then she phrases the idea *in her own words*.
- Teases James affectionately: "You're doing that thing again where you fall in love with your own explanation."

**Pedagogical superpower — Teach-Back:** Once per episode, near the end, Emma explains the central idea back in her own words, and James only gently corrects if needed. This is the listener's anchoring moment.

**What Emma never does:** Doesn't only ask — she also contributes her own insights (life connections, implications); doesn't play dumb; doesn't say "interesting" without explaining why.

---

## Dynamic Rules Between the Two

1. **Speaking ratio:** Roughly 60% James / 40% Emma in explanation sections, and reverses in openings, guess moments, and teach-back. Emma must not disappear for a full minute.
2. **Turn length:** Most turns are 1–3 sentences. Hard ceiling: 4 sentences in a row — then the other must enter (question, reaction, or [interrupting]).
3. **Interruption budget:** 1–3 real interruptions ([interrupting]) per episode. More — annoying; fewer — sounds like alternating narration.
4. **Guess protocol:** James poses a question → Emma guesses out loud (wrong half the time, in a *plausible* way) → James doesn't say a dry "no" but "Ah, that's what everyone thinks! But look what happens..." → the reveal.
5. **No serial agreement:** Chains of "right" / "exactly" / "totally" are forbidden. At most one affirmation, then new content must be added.
6. **Humor:** Comes from the situation and the dynamic (guesses, teasing, analogies that fall apart) — never planted jokes. Rule of thumb: if the joke survives outside context, it's probably planted.
7. **Warmth:** Beneath all the jabs — two people who like each other and are crazy about the material. Cynicism is never directed at the listener or at mathematics.
8. Names: dont mention their names during the dialog in the podcast.

## Benchmark Example (from Episode 1 — "What Is a Matrix")

```text
James: Okay Emma, question. You open Excel. What do you see?
Emma: Um... cells? Lots of empty cells judging me.
James: [laughs] The cells that judge you — that's exactly it. Hold on to that picture.
Emma: Wait, you're telling me the scary thing called a "matrix"... is Excel?
James: I'm saying you've been using matrices since ninth grade, only nobody told you.
Emma: [suspicious] Okay... I smell a trick. Give me an example. Now.
James: With pleasure. A movie theater. Four rows, three seats in each row.
Emma: Sad theater, but fine.
James: [chuckles] Indie theater. Now — your friend saves you a seat. She writes: "seat two". You find her?
Emma: Um... no. There's a seat two in every row. That's... four seat twos?
James: And there it is. The moment you invented the reason a matrix needs two numbers.
Emma: [surprised] Wait, that's it?? Row and seat?!
```

Notice what the example demonstrates: short turns, guessing, humor from the situation, zero jargon, and the idea born from need — not from a definition.
